Reading 2

Chapter 3

The section “Negotiable Body” provides three different examples that show how our senses can trick our perception. The first experiment involves the subject extending their hand and another person must tap the subject’s nose. The subject starts to feel as if their nose is two feet long. The second experiment has the subject place one hand under a desktop. Another person taps the subject’s visible hand and the surface of the desktop. The hidden hand will feel the sensation of being tapped. The third experiment is similar to the previous one, except it involves using a fake hand. In all three of these examples, the mind deceives the subject’s consciousness. Our brains support highly negotiable body images of ourselves. The section “Neural Opportunism” explains how our brains process images differently than how they actually exist. Our brains tend to omit information that is not relevant to the task at hand. Because of this reason, many things tend to get overlooked. The reading suggests that our visual brains are opportunistic, meaning they are ready to make decisions based on the fragments of information they put together. The section “Word Brains” discusses how the brain processes topics such as linguistics. The reading provides an example of a group of chimpanzees and how they were able to cognitively associate a shape of a plastic token with a pair or pairs of objects. Their brains were able to connect lower-order concepts (sameness and difference) with physical objects. Our brains are capable of matching patterns, sensory responses, and spatial awareness. However, our brains do not excel at logical processes such as deciphering binary code the way a computer does. The section “Scaffolded Thinking” talks about how our brains use aids and props to strengthen the weaknesses mentioned in the previous section. One example pointed out in this section is an artist using sketches to map out a drawing due to the constraints of human thought. The final section, “building better brains”, explains how our brains improve as they engage in new experiences.

Meta-learning focuses on exploiting information. The term “meta” is often used in the gaming community when referring to techniques that will allow the user to exploit game mechanics. Constructive learning focuses on building structures that can be used to aid in learning. The brain is building an environment to strengthen the absorption of information.
